01

Strategy & positioning

The strategic read everything else flows from. The identity, voice, and messaging in this book all serve these decisions.

The category problem

Mid-market companies adopted AI faster than they organized for it. Their assistants answer fluently — and often wrongly — about internal processes, because no one curated the ground truth, and what's written down rarely matches how the company actually runs. That gap is where confidence in AI quietly breaks.

The strategic wedge

Other "AI for work" tools are retrieval engines — they find what already exists in your files. RunOil does what they can't: it captures truth that lives in no document, reconciles conflicting versions of it, and stays model-neutral by design. It doesn't compete with your AI. It makes your AI worth trusting.

Positioning statement

For mid-market organizations adopting AI, RunOil is the neutral system of record for organizational truth — the Atomic Truth Engine. It captures, reconciles, and governs how the company actually works into a governed Corpus — so every AI tool answers with an executive's command of the business, not a new hire's guesswork.

Personality
  • Trustworthy & neutral
  • Clear & rigorous
  • Calm — a steadying presence
  • Confident, never enterprise-stiff
Role of the brand

In an over-hyped category, credibility is the product. The brand earns trust by behaving like infrastructure — precise, governed, quietly authoritative. Something a company would stake its operating truth on.

The hidden audience

The consultant network is the engine. The brand's second job: make independent partners proud to put it in front of their own clients. Credibility is what they're reselling.

02

The name

RunOil is validated. The new mark resolves the one risk the name carried — and does it without a single drop of literal oil.

↗ Why the name works

  • You own runoil.ai — clean, category-right, no acquisition cost.
  • Companies run; the name is short, concrete, and verb-like. It says what it does.
  • The split wordmark — Run leaning forward, the mark as the "O", il settled — turns the name into the product story: motion reconciled into something stable.

⚠ The risk we designed out

  • Unframed, "oil" can read petroleum, or worse, brush against "snake oil" — fatal for an anti-hype trust brand.
  • The fix: the identity never depicts oil. The mark carries a reconciliation meaning instead, so the name reads as infrastructure, not crude.
Verdict

Keep RunOil. Let the mark carry the meaning.

The diagonal-split mark — two sources snapping into one truth — gives the name a meaning that has nothing to do with petroleum and everything to do with what RunOil does. If you ever want to revisit the name, Plumbline, Datum, and Throughline remain credible alternates. But the equity, the domain, and now the mark all point one way: keep it.

06

Voice & tone

We sound like a sharp advisor who respects the reader's time and intelligence. No hype, no buzzwords, no fear. We name the problem honestly and explain how we solve it.

Direct

Always lead with the point. Short sentences carry weight.

Never hedge or pad.

Rigorous

Always back claims with evidence — sources, reconciled facts, numbers.

Never use AI buzzwords ("revolutionary," "magic," "supercharge").

Steady

Always stay calm — the adult in an over-hyped room.

Never fear-monger. We relieve anxiety; we don't sell it.

07

The lexicon

These terms are canonical. Using them consistently across product, sales, and brand is itself a trust signal — and it's how we hold the line that RunOil is a truth & governance layer, not search.

The product
Atomic Truth Engine

How the RunOil SaaS is positioned — the engine that captures, reconciles, governs, and publishes the Corpus. The product partners build their practice on.

Corpus

The living, governed body of Atomic Truths — the system of record. Plain-text, versioned, AI-organized, always inspectable. Never a "wiki," "knowledge base," or "database."

Atomic Truth

The verified unit of organizational truth — one typed, sourced, owned fact about how the company works. Types: Process, Decision, Friction, Open Loop, Ownership, Definition-of-Done.

Reality Gap

The delta between what leadership states, what staff live, and what the documents claim. RunOil's signature output — the thing retrieval tools can't produce.

The method & the people
the Guide

The coaching assistant that runs Discovery Sessions on the interviewer's screen. "A teleprompter for objectives, not a call-center script." Lowercase "the," capital "G."

Discovery Session

A recorded, processed coaching call that captures truth into the Corpus.

Configure Group

The Partner plus client champions who configure and govern an instance.

Partner

The independent consultant who delivers RunOil engagements — our front line and distribution channel.

Customer of Record

The company that pays for and owns its RunOil instance and its Corpus.

Consumption / Governance Plane

Where AI tools read governed truth (consumption) vs. where truth is defined, verified, and published (governance).

The plumbing
MCP

Model Context Protocol — the open standard RunOil uses to feed governed context to any AI tool.

Agent Skills

The open standard for packaging procedures an AI can execute. With MCP, this is how the Corpus reaches every model.

Say this, not that

Words we don't use

wiki · knowledge base · enterprise search · knowledge assistant
→ Corpus, the system of record
documents · notes · entries
→ Atomic Truths
monitoring · tracking · oversight
→ governance, at org / policy level only
Usage & capitalization
  • Capitalize the canonical nouns: Atomic Truth, Corpus, Reality Gap, Discovery Session, Partner, Customer of Record.
  • RunOil is a truth & governance layer — never "search" or a "knowledge assistant."
  • RunOil publishes into Claude, ChatGPT, and Grok — it never "replaces" or "competes with" them.
  • Governance is always at the org / policy / cost level — never individual monitoring.

Visual language

Engineered to feel like governed infrastructure — and to actively dodge the two traps: AI clichés and any whiff of surveillance.

Imagery
  • Structure, systems, things engineered to fit — echoing the split mark.
  • Honest, candid workplaces — people shown on the employee's side, never observed or tracked.
  • Clean navy/zinc compositions, generous white space.
  • Never: glowing orbs, neural swirls, brains, robots, or anyone being "monitored."
Iconography & motif

Thin, even strokes. The diagonal seam recurs as the connective thread — two parts meeting at a clean line.

Layout principles
  • Rounded cards (16px), zinc hairline borders, generous spacing.
  • Navy carries structure; violet appears once per view.
  • Mono labels mark sections like instrument readouts.
  • Calm density — detail only where data earns it.

The brand decision filter

Before anything ships — a slide, a page, an email, a deck — run it through these. Inconsistency is the silent brand killer.

Before publishing, ask

Five questions

  1. Does it sound direct, rigorous, and steady — no hype, no fear?
  2. Does it feel like governed infrastructure, not another AI box?
  3. Is violet the only accent, used once?
  4. Could a skeptical buyer and a proud partner both stand behind it?
  5. Is there any trace of surveillance or "watching"? If yes, kill it.
Consistency checklist
  • Navy / violet / lilac / zinc only — no stray hues.
  • Inter throughout; mono only for metadata.
  • Mark: navy leads, violet completes — gap intact, never reversed.
  • Tone matches the context table in §06.
  • Imagery is honest and employee-side — never monitoring.
  • Claims are evidence-led — sourced, reconciled, or cut.

One off-brand touchpoint erodes the trust ten on-brand ones build. In a trust category, that's the whole game.
